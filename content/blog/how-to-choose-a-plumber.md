# How to Choose a Plumber: A Practical Guide for Homeowners

When a pipe bursts or your water heater stops working, the pressure to hire someone fast can cloud your judgment. But choosing the wrong plumber can turn a manageable problem into an expensive disaster. Taking even a few minutes to evaluate your options properly can save you hundreds of dollars and a lot of frustration.

This guide walks you through exactly what to look for when choosing a plumber, whether you have time to plan ahead or need help urgently.

## Start With Licensing

The single most important thing to verify before hiring any plumber is whether they hold a valid license in your state or province. Plumbing work affects your water supply, your drainage system, and in some cases your home's structural integrity. Licensed plumbers have passed exams, completed apprenticeships, and are held to a professional standard.

Most states and provinces have an online license lookup tool. A quick search for your state's contractor licensing board will get you there. Enter the plumber's name or business name and confirm their license is current and in good standing.

An unlicensed plumber may be cheaper upfront, but if something goes wrong, you could face repair costs, failed inspections, and trouble with your homeowner's insurance.

## Verify Insurance Before Anyone Enters Your Home

Beyond licensing, your plumber should carry two types of insurance: general liability and workers' compensation.

General liability protects your property if the plumber damages something while working. Workers' compensation covers the plumber and any employees if they are injured on your property. Without workers' comp, you could potentially be held responsible for medical costs if a worker is hurt in your home.

Ask the plumber directly for proof of insurance, or ask them to have their insurer send you a certificate of insurance. Any reputable plumber will have this ready without hesitation.

## Check Online Reviews Carefully

Online reviews are useful, but they require some interpretation. A plumber with 200 reviews averaging 4.6 stars is almost always a safer choice than one with 12 reviews averaging 5.0 stars. A large review count tells you the business has real volume and a consistent track record.

When reading reviews, pay attention to the specifics. Positive reviews that mention punctuality, clear communication, honest pricing, and clean work are more meaningful than generic praise. Negative reviews that mention surprise charges, incomplete work, or no-shows are serious warning signs.

Also look at how the business responds to negative reviews. A professional response that acknowledges the concern and offers to make it right is a good sign. Defensive or dismissive responses suggest the same attitude you would get on the job.

## Get at Least Two Quotes

For any non-emergency job, getting two or three quotes is always worth doing. This gives you a sense of the fair market rate for your area and your specific job, and it protects you from being significantly overcharged.

When comparing quotes, make sure each plumber is quoting the same scope of work. Ask each one to explain what they plan to do, what parts they will use, and what the quote includes. A lower quote that uses inferior parts or skips important steps may cost more in the long run.

## Ask These Questions Before You Hire

A few direct questions can tell you a lot about a plumber's professionalism:

**Do you pull permits for this type of work?** Some jobs legally require a permit, and a plumber who skips permits to save time is cutting a corner that could affect your home's resale value and insurance coverage.

**Who will actually do the work?** Some plumbing companies use subcontractors. There's nothing wrong with that, but you want to know who will be in your home and whether that person is also licensed and insured.

**What is your warranty on parts and labor?** A confident plumber stands behind their work. One year on labor is common; parts warranties depend on the manufacturer.

**How do you handle unexpected issues?** If the job is more complex than it appeared, will they call you before proceeding, or will they just do the work and bill you?

## Red Flags to Watch For

A few warning signs should make you pause or walk away entirely:

- Demanding full payment in cash before the job begins
- Unable or unwilling to provide license or insurance information
- No physical address, only a cell phone number
- Pressure to decide immediately without time to get a second opinion
- Quotes that are dramatically lower than everyone else (often a sign something is being left out)

## Trust Your Instincts

After checking the practical boxes, trust how the interaction feels. Did they answer your questions clearly and respectfully? Did they show up when they said they would? Did they explain what they found and what they recommend without being condescending?

A good plumber communicates well. That matters just as much as technical skill, because you need to understand what is happening in your own home and feel confident in the decision you are making.

Taking fifteen minutes to verify a license, read a handful of reviews, and ask a few straightforward questions is the difference between a job well done and a problem that gets worse.
