# Licensed vs. Unlicensed Plumber: Why It Matters More Than You Think

The difference in price between a licensed plumber and someone advertising cheap work online can look tempting, especially for a job that seems simple. But plumbing work carries real risk — to your property, your health, and your finances — and the licensing system exists for good reason.

Here is what actually separates a licensed plumber from an unlicensed one, and why that distinction matters for homeowners.

## What Licensing Actually Means

Becoming a licensed plumber is not a quick process. In most states and provinces, it requires completing a formal apprenticeship that lasts four to five years, working thousands of hours under the supervision of a licensed journeyman or master plumber, and then passing a written exam that tests knowledge of plumbing codes, safety standards, and technical practices.

Many states have multiple license tiers. An apprentice works under supervision. A journeyman can work independently. A master plumber can supervise others, pull permits, and run a plumbing business. Each step requires documented experience and passing an exam.

When you hire a licensed plumber, you are hiring someone who has demonstrated competence through a structured, verified process. When you hire an unlicensed plumber, you have no way to verify their training, experience, or knowledge of code requirements.

## The Insurance Problem

Licensed plumbers are typically required to carry general liability insurance and, if they have employees, workers' compensation insurance. These requirements are part of maintaining a license in most jurisdictions.

Unlicensed plumbers often carry no insurance at all. This creates serious exposure for you as a homeowner.

If an uninsured plumber damages your water line, floods your basement, or cracks a foundation wall, you will be dealing with that repair cost yourself. Your homeowner's insurance may not cover damage caused by unpermitted or unlicensed work.

If an uninsured worker is injured in your home, you could face a personal injury claim against you directly. Homeowner's liability coverage has limits, and those limits can be exceeded quickly in a serious injury.

## Permit Requirements and Code Compliance

Many plumbing jobs require a permit. Water heater replacements, new fixture installations, changes to drain lines, and water service work are common examples. Permits require inspections, and inspections ensure the work meets local building codes.

A licensed plumber knows what requires a permit, knows how to obtain one, and is accountable to the inspection process. An unlicensed plumber typically skips permits entirely, either because they do not want the scrutiny or because they do not qualify to pull permits in the first place.

Unpermitted plumbing work can create real problems for you:

**Home sales:** When you sell your home, unpermitted work is often discovered during the buyer's inspection. This can delay or kill a sale, or require you to bring the work up to code at your own expense.

**Insurance claims:** If your water heater fails and causes water damage, your insurance company may investigate whether the installation was permitted. An unpermitted installation can give them grounds to deny the claim.

**Liability:** If unpermitted plumbing causes injury or property damage to a neighbor, you may bear personal liability for the consequences.

## What Happens When Something Goes Wrong

When you hire a licensed plumber and something goes wrong, you have recourse. You can file a complaint with the state licensing board. You can pursue the matter through their insurance. In serious cases, their license can be suspended or revoked.

When something goes wrong with an unlicensed plumber, your options are far more limited. Many will simply disappear. You cannot file a licensing complaint against someone who has no license. Their lack of insurance means there is no coverage to pursue. You may end up paying twice — once for the original job and once to a licensed plumber to fix what went wrong.

## When the Savings Are Not Real

The appeal of an unlicensed plumber is almost always price. They often quote less because their overhead is lower — no insurance premiums, no licensing fees, no continuing education requirements.

But consider the full picture. If an unlicensed plumber does a job incorrectly, the cost of repair typically exceeds the original savings several times over. If they damage your property and have no insurance, you absorb the full cost. If their work fails an inspection you didn't know was needed, you pay a licensed plumber to redo the work properly.

The savings are frequently an illusion.

## How to Verify a License

Most states and provinces maintain an online license verification tool through the contractor licensing board or department of consumer affairs. Search for your state or province name plus "plumber license lookup" and you will typically find it within the first few results.

Enter the plumber's name or business name. Confirm the license type, that it is current and not expired, and that there are no disciplinary actions on record.

This takes two minutes and is one of the most valuable two minutes you will spend before any significant plumbing job.

---

For a minor job with a trusted neighbor who happens to know plumbing, your risk tolerance is your own decision. But for anything that touches your main water line, your sewer system, or any fixture that requires a permit, the case for hiring a licensed, insured plumber is very strong. The licensing system exists because the risks of getting it wrong are real.
