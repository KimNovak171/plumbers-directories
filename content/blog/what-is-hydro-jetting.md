# What Is Hydro Jetting and When Do You Need It?

If you have had a plumber deal with a stubborn drain clog, you may have heard the term hydro jetting. It is not as common as drain snaking, but in certain situations it is significantly more effective. Understanding what it is and when it is the appropriate solution helps you have an informed conversation with your plumber and make a confident decision.

## What Is Hydro Jetting?

Hydro jetting is a drain cleaning method that uses a high-pressure stream of water to scour the inside of pipes and remove blockages, buildup, and debris. A specialized machine pumps water at very high pressure — typically 1,500 to 4,000 psi — through a flexible hose with a specially designed nozzle at the end.

The nozzle has jets that point both forward (to break up blockages ahead of it) and backward at an angle (which propels the nozzle through the pipe and simultaneously cleans the pipe walls as it advances). The result is a pipe that is not just cleared of a specific clog but thoroughly cleaned along its length.

The dislodged material — grease, scale, debris, roots — is flushed downstream and out of the system.

## How It Differs From Drain Snaking

A drain snake (or auger) is a rotating cable that is fed into a pipe to break through or retrieve a specific clog. It is the right tool for many situations, but it has limitations:

- A snake punches through a clog but does not clean the pipe walls around it. Grease and scale coating the inside of the pipe remains.
- A snake cannot remove all types of blockages equally well. Heavy scale or root masses may be pushed through rather than fully cleared.
- The clog tends to return faster after snaking because the buildup that contributed to it is still present.

Hydro jetting addresses all of these limitations. Instead of punching through a clog, it scours the pipe from wall to wall, removing not just the immediate blockage but the conditions that led to it.

## When Is Hydro Jetting the Right Choice?

**Recurring drain blockages.** If the same drain clogs repeatedly despite snaking, it is likely because the underlying buildup has not been fully removed. Hydro jetting clears the pipe more completely, which often resolves persistent recurrence.

**Grease buildup in kitchen drain lines.** Grease accumulation in kitchen pipes does not respond well to snaking. Hydro jetting cuts through and flushes grease from the pipe walls in a way that snaking cannot.

**Sewer line scale and mineral buildup.** In older homes with cast iron or steel pipes, mineral scale can narrow the pipe diameter significantly over decades. Hydro jetting can restore a significant portion of the original flow capacity.

**Root intrusion (in some cases).** Tree roots that have infiltrated a sewer line can sometimes be flushed out with hydro jetting, particularly if the intrusion is not yet severe. However, if roots have caused significant damage to the pipe itself, the pipe will need repair or replacement regardless of cleaning.

**Pre-inspection cleaning.** Before a camera inspection of a sewer line, hydro jetting is sometimes used to clean the pipe walls so the camera can get a clear view of the pipe's condition.

**Commercial and restaurant applications.** High-volume kitchen drains that accumulate grease rapidly are frequently maintained with scheduled hydro jetting.

## When Hydro Jetting Is Not Appropriate

Hydro jetting uses very high water pressure and is not suitable in all situations:

**Older or fragile pipes.** Very old clay pipes, deteriorated cast iron, or pipes already showing damage may not withstand the pressure. A camera inspection to assess pipe condition is typically done before hydro jetting an older sewer line.

**Simple clogs that a snake can clear.** For a straightforward blockage caused by hair, a foreign object, or a soap mass in a fixture drain, a snake is the appropriate and less expensive tool. Hydro jetting is not necessary for every clog.

**Pipes with significant existing damage.** If a pipe has cracks, offsets, or extensive root damage, hydro jetting can make the damage worse. Camera inspection first determines whether the pipe can safely handle the treatment.

## What to Expect During the Service

A plumber performing hydro jetting will access the pipe through a clean-out port — typically a capped access point in the drain line. They will feed the jetting hose into the line, then advance it through the pipe with the high-pressure jets running.

The process for a main sewer line typically takes one to two hours. Smaller interior drain lines take less time.

In most cases, a plumber will perform a camera inspection either before or after jetting to assess the condition of the pipe and confirm the results.

## Cost of Hydro Jetting

Hydro jetting costs more than snaking because it requires specialized equipment and takes more time. For a main sewer line, costs typically range from $300 to $600 or more, depending on the length of the line and the severity of the buildup. Interior drain lines are typically less.

While the upfront cost is higher than snaking, hydro jetting often provides a longer-lasting result — particularly for recurrent clogs caused by grease or scale — which can make it more cost-effective over time.

---

Hydro jetting is a powerful tool that belongs in certain situations and not others. A plumber who recommends it should be able to explain why it is appropriate for your specific situation over a simpler alternative. When it is the right call, it is one of the most thorough drain cleaning solutions available.
